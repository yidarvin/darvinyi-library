## The thesis

Christensen's puzzle is uncomfortable precisely because it does not blame failure on
laziness, bad managers, or a missed memo. A well-run company listens to its customers,
improves the product they buy, and directs capital toward attractive margins. Those are
sound habits inside an established market. Yet they can leave an opening for an entrant
whose first offer is cheaper, simpler, and initially worse on the measures that matter to
the mainstream. The entrant learns where the leader is not looking, improves, and eventually
makes its modest beginning consequential.

## Why it matters

The word "disruptive" is often used for any impressive launch. That makes it a poor tool
for strategy. The book is more useful as a question about incentives: which customers shape
our roadmap, which jobs are becoming over-served, and what small market would be invisible
if we judged it by the economics of the core business? The answers can reveal a threat, but
they can also reveal a sensible reason not to chase every novelty.

## Good management can create a blind spot

The familiar customer is loud for a good reason. They pay today, know what better looks
like, and can tell a supplier where the current product falls short. A company that reliably
acts on that information gets better at serving the profitable center of its market. The
same feedback system can make a tiny, low-margin opportunity look irrational. It is not
being ignored because no one noticed it. It is being filtered out because the existing
organization has learned what a worthwhile opportunity normally looks like.

## Performance can outrun what most buyers need

In a mature category, suppliers often keep raising performance faster than many customers
can use it. More capacity, precision, features, or service may still help a demanding
segment, but can become surplus for someone with a simpler job. That gap is where a less
capable product can be useful. Its weakness is real, not a marketing trick. It wins because
it changes another part of the trade: price, convenience, access, or ease of use. The
question is not whether the newcomer is better overall. It is whether it is good enough for
a group whose needs the leading offer has passed.

## A foothold is not a frontal assault

An entrant rarely begins by beating the leader for its most demanding customers. It starts
where the incumbent has weak incentives to respond. A low-end foothold serves customers who
are paying for more than they need. A new-market foothold serves people who previously could
not participate at all. Both can look too small or too unattractive to merit the core
organization's attention. A high-end challenger that immediately targets the incumbent's
best customers is a different competitive problem, often a sustaining contest where the
incumbent's resources are an advantage.

## Margins quietly direct attention

When the low end leaves, an incumbent may feel relief before it feels danger. Losing a
small, demanding-to-serve account can make the remaining business look more profitable.
Managers then invest in the customers with greater willingness to pay, while the entrant
gets room to improve. This pattern explains why a warning alone is not enough. An employee
can spot the emerging offer and still be unable to win resources for it when every familiar
measure says the core deserves the budget. The dilemma is built into the comparison, not
into any one person's foresight.

## The new business needs a fitting home

A venture aimed at a small foothold cannot be judged as though it were already the core.
It may need smaller revenue targets, different channels, a different cost structure, and
permission to learn from customers the main business does not know. Christensen's practical
answer is organizational separation with connection: give the effort enough independence to
fit its market, while using the parent selectively for assets that truly help. Separation is
not a magic wall. It is a way to stop a mature operating model from rejecting a young one
before the young one has had a chance to find its economics.

## The Model

Disruptive innovation is a process, not a badge for a company or a technology. An entrant
finds a foothold that the established offer handles poorly or expensively. It builds a
business that can live there, improves the offer along dimensions that eventually matter to
more customers, and moves into the market the incumbent has been refining. At each stage,
the incumbent's ordinary incentives can make retreat toward its profitable core look more
reasonable than a fight. The model describes that asymmetric path, not a guaranteed outcome.

## Put it to work

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Map the customers your product may be over-serving">
For one important product, list the performance measures your best customers request. Then
interview a smaller or less profitable group about the job they are trying to finish. Look
for a feature, service level, or cost they would willingly trade away for simpler access.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Test for a real foothold">
Write down the group's alternative today. Is it an expensive version of the product, a
clumsy workaround, or no participation at all? If the answer is only that they prefer a
better premium product, the opportunity may be sustaining rather than disruptive.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Give the experiment its own scorecard">
Choose three early measures that fit the new market, such as repeat use, a reachable price,
or the cost of serving the first cohort. Keep them separate from the core's revenue hurdle
until the venture has enough evidence to justify a larger comparison.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Decide the response before the threat is urgent">
For a plausible foothold, name the asset the core can contribute and the constraint it must
not impose. Set a small budget, an owner, and a review date. The goal is not to predict every
winner. It is to create a way for a strange opportunity to produce evidence before the core
economics dismiss it.
</ExerciseCard>

## Where people get it wrong

<Callout kind="note">
Disruptive is not a synonym for novel, fast-growing, or technologically sophisticated. A
product that begins by outperforming the incumbent for its best customers is usually a
sustaining challenger, even if it later wins. The theory also does not make failure
inevitable: incumbents can respond, entrants can stall, and industry history rarely follows
one clean curve. Treat it as a disciplined way to inspect a competitive path, not as a label
that explains any market change after the fact.
</Callout>

## If you remember one thing

The dangerous competitor may begin with an offer your best customers do not want. Watch for
the people your current economics make easy to ignore, then give a plausible foothold enough
room to show whether it can grow.

Good to Great examines how a mature company builds disciplined momentum, while The Lean
Startup offers a method for testing the small, uncertain market a new venture may need to
learn from. Zero to One complements this page by asking which businesses can create a new
