## The thesis

Taleb asks us to distrust a result that arrives without its denominator. A winning trader,
founder, or forecaster may be skilled. They may also be the one visible survivor of many
people who took a similar risk and lost. When success is the only evidence that reaches us,
luck wears the costume of ability. The practical task is to separate a process from one
outcome, seek the missing failures, and avoid exposures that one fortunate run can hide.

## Why it matters

Modern work is unusually good at broadcasting wins. Rankings, case studies, return charts,
and social feeds preserve the people who made it through a contest. The failed peers often
leave no book, interview, or archive. That creates a lopsided education. We inherit the
winner's habits, then mistake their visibility for proof that those habits caused the win.
The error matters most when the cost of copying a strategy is large or irreversible.

## Key ideas

### A visible winner is a filtered observation

Suppose a large group makes risky choices. A few will land a remarkable result even if their
choices are no better than anyone else's. Attention collects around those few, because an
extreme success is memorable and a quiet failure is not. The sample that reaches us has
already passed through a filter. Before treating its traits as a recipe, ask who tried
something similar, how many there were, and what became of the ones who did not survive.

### A streak is evidence with a noisy source

A run of good outcomes should raise interest, not end the inquiry. In a field with many
participants and plenty of variation, somebody will have an impressive run by chance.
Repeated results can contain information about skill, but their meaning depends on the
number of trials, the baseline odds, the risks taken, and whether the same method works in
new conditions. A streak becomes more informative when it survives a fair comparison, not
when it is retold more confidently.

### Consequences matter more than the story of the win

Two strategies can produce the same average-looking result while taking radically different
risks. One earns steadily while preserving the ability to continue after a bad surprise. The
other collects small wins by accepting a rare loss large enough to end the game. A short
record may flatter the second strategy because the damaging branch has not arrived yet.
The sound question is not only, "How often did this work?" It is also, "What happens on the
bad branch, and can the person or system still act afterward?"

### Stories arrive after the outcome

Once the winner is known, the mind can assemble a clean account of why they deserved to
win. Their confidence becomes vision, their timing becomes judgment, and the risks that
could have ended differently fade into background detail. A story can still teach. It needs
to be treated as a hypothesis rather than a verdict. The useful version asks which parts
were decisions, which conditions were outside the person's control, and which alternatives
would have led to another ending.

### A process earns trust across different draws

The antidote is neither cynicism nor a demand for impossible certainty. It is a better
audit. State the strategy before the outcome, name the risk it carries, compare it with
alternatives, and return to it across enough independent situations to see whether it holds
up. Look for behavior that would remain sensible even when the favorable draw does not
arrive. This turns admiration into a testable question and lets evidence accumulate without
pretending randomness has vanished.

## The Model

Survivorship bias begins with a population, not a winner. Many people may use an approach,
but only some remain visible after outcomes and attrition have done their sorting. If we
study the survivors alone, the sample makes survival look like proof of the approach. The
missing group is not a footnote. It is the information needed to tell whether the apparent
success came from a durable edge, a favorable draw, or some combination of both.

## Put it to work

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Ask for the denominator">
When a success story offers a tactic, ask how many people used a similar tactic and what
happened to them. If that number is unavailable, treat the story as a lead for further
research, not as evidence that a method works.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Read past the headline metric">
For an investment, project, or vendor, look beyond the best-looking return, launch, or case
study. Ask about the worst period, the conditions that produced it, the losses avoided, and
the risks that remain outside the chart.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Write the process before the result">
Before a meaningful decision, record the goal, the assumptions, the downside you can bear,
and what evidence would make you change course. A short record prevents a later win or loss
from rewriting what you actually believed.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Prefer experiments that keep you in the game">
When uncertainty is high, favor a small reversible test over a large one-way commitment.
Set an exposure limit before you begin. The aim is to give good ideas more than one chance
to prove themselves, rather than letting a single unlucky draw decide everything.
</ExerciseCard>

## Where people get it wrong

<Callout kind="note">
Luck is not a claim that skill does not exist. Expertise can be real, and careful evidence
can detect it. The correction is about the strength of the inference: a winner's result
alone cannot tell us how much came from ability, chance, selection, or a risk that has not
yet paid its cost. Survivorship bias also is not an excuse to ignore performance. Over a
well-defined comparison set and enough relevant trials, performance remains useful evidence.
The discipline is to keep looking for the denominator and the downside before treating a
pleasant record as a durable law.
</Callout>

## If you remember one thing

Do not ask only why the winner succeeded. Ask who tried the same thing, who disappeared,
and what the winner risked on the path. A result becomes useful evidence when it is placed
back into the full field that produced it.
