## The thesis

Every consequential choice is a wager on incomplete information: state what you believe,
why, and what would revise it. Judge its process by the information available before the
result, then use that result to improve the next decision rather than treating it as a verdict.

## Why it matters

Outcomes have a persuasive force. A profitable launch can make a reckless plan look wise,
while a sound medical, financial, or hiring decision can be followed by bad news. That
tempts people to turn every result into a verdict on their own intelligence. Duke's poker
frame is useful because it makes the missing ingredient hard to ignore: chance. A record of
how a decision was made gives luck somewhere to sit besides the driver's seat.

## Key ideas

### An outcome is evidence, not a grade

A result matters, but it cannot carry the whole evaluation. Good information, sensible
tradeoffs, and a sound estimate can still lead to a loss when the unlikely branch occurs.
The reverse is also true: a decision made from wishful assumptions can be rescued by a lucky
break. Treating the result as the entire report card teaches the wrong lesson in both cases.
Review the reasons, alternatives, and probabilities that existed at the moment of choice,
then let the outcome add evidence to that record.

### A belief should have a price

Saying that something is likely can hide a wide range of confidence. A bet forces the
question into the open: how likely, what would you give up, and what information would make
you change your mind? The point is not to gamble on every decision. It is to treat a belief
as a claim with a strength, a downside, and a condition for revision. Once the odds are
explicit, two people can disagree usefully instead of arguing over the same vague word.

### Facts and stories need different labels

The mind does not enjoy an empty explanation. A missed target becomes proof that the team
was careless, a win becomes proof that the strategy was right, and a confident narrative
fills the gap between what happened and what caused it. Some of those stories will be true.
The trouble begins when an interpretation is filed as a fact. Keep the observable event,
your inference, and the uncertainty around it separate. That small act makes it easier to
notice another explanation and harder to defend a flattering one.

### Learning needs a record made before you know

Memory is a poor audit trail. Once a result is known, people quietly remember having been
more certain, more skeptical, or more prescient than they were. A decision journal gives the
past a voice. Before acting, write the choice, the odds, the reasons, the alternatives, and
what would count as a surprise. When the result arrives, compare it with that frozen record.
The goal is not self-punishment. It is a feedback loop sturdy enough to reveal recurring
overconfidence, missing information, and good judgment that met bad luck.

### Dissent is information to investigate

Agreement feels efficient, especially after a group has committed to a plan. But quick
consensus can hide the very uncertainty a decision needs to expose. A productive group asks
for independent views before the senior voice sets the tone, welcomes a reasoned challenge,
and asks what evidence would change each position. The aim is not permanent argument. It is
to find the assumptions that only one person noticed while there is still time to act on
them.

### Rehearse the future in both directions

Before a decision, imagine that the plan failed and write several plausible reasons. This
premortem turns a vague feeling of risk into assumptions you can test, safeguards you can
add, or triggers you can monitor. Then imagine a successful future and work backward from
it: what had to become true, and which early signs would show that the path is forming? The
two exercises do not predict the future. They widen the range of futures considered before
commitment makes the preferred story feel inevitable.

## The Model

Resulting is Duke's name for a common shortcut: using a good or bad outcome to decide whether
the preceding choice was good or bad. It feels natural because the outcome is visible and
the decision conditions have passed. The correction is not to ignore outcomes. It is to put
them in the right order. First reconstruct the information, options, probabilities, and
stakes available before the choice. Then use the eventual result as one new data point for
the next decision, rather than as a retroactive rewrite of the old one.

## Put it to work

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Write one decision before you make it">
For a decision with real consequences, record the options, your current best choice, the two
strongest reasons, and the chance you think it works. Include one fact that would make you
reverse the choice. Keep the note short enough that you will actually use it.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Ask the bet question">
When someone says a claim is obvious, ask what odds they would put on it and what evidence
would move them. Apply the same question to yourself. The goal is not a clever challenge. It
is a more specific belief that another person can inspect.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Run a ten-minute premortem">
Before a launch, hire, purchase, or commitment, suppose it has gone badly six months from
now. List three ways that could have happened. Circle the one you can cheaply reduce now,
and choose an early warning sign for another.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Review without a victory lap or a trial">
When a decision resolves, open the original note before explaining the result. Ask what the
process got right, what it missed, and whether chance had a large role. Change one part of
the next decision process, even if this one happened to go well.
</ExerciseCard>

## Where people get it wrong

<Callout kind="note">
Thinking in bets does not mean reducing every human choice to a monetary wager or refusing
to take responsibility for consequences. Some decisions involve duties, relationships, and
hard constraints that a probability alone cannot settle. Nor does uncertainty excuse a weak
process. Known risks still deserve preparation, and repeated losses still deserve a serious
look at the choices that produced them. The useful distinction is between a single noisy
result and a pattern. Better process raises the odds over many decisions; it never creates a
guarantee for one of them.
</Callout>

## If you remember one thing

Do not let one win teach you to repeat a bad decision, or one loss teach you to discard a
good one. Preserve what you knew before acting, then let each result improve your next bet.
