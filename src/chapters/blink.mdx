## The thesis

Snap judgments can sometimes extract a useful pattern from very little information, but the same
fast response can mistake familiarity, fear, or stereotype for evidence. Treat the instant as an
impression, then use deliberate checks and later outcomes to decide whether it deserves trust.

## Why it matters

Many choices arrive before a spreadsheet or meeting can: an interviewer senses a poor fit, a
clinician sees something off, or a coach spots a mechanical error. The first feeling is neither a
private oracle nor a reason to drown in detail. Notice it, ask what could make it informative,
then build a way to challenge it.

## Key ideas

### Fast is not the same as careless

A rapid judgment can reflect repeated exposure that makes useful cues easy to recognize, or a
cue unrelated to the question. Time does not distinguish the two; the cue's relation to the
outcome does.

### A thin slice is narrow, not complete

Thin-slicing describes a rapid impression formed from a brief sample, not a conscious method for
sorting signals from noise. Research on brief observations suggests that a small nonverbal sample
can sometimes preserve information about a wider interaction, while accuracy still varies across
the judgment and the observer. A later cue audit can test that impression, but it happens outside
the thin slice itself.

### Experience gives intuition its vocabulary

Experts often cannot narrate every feature behind a recognition because practice has bundled
many comparisons into one felt response. That does not make their knowledge mystical. It means
their learning was specific: they saw outcomes, noticed errors, and encountered the same class of
problem again. A radiologist, a firefighter, and a tennis coach may all develop fast reads, but
only inside the boundaries of the feedback their work actually supplies.

### More analysis can protect a decision, or obscure its signal

Some choices reward a pause. Others suffer when a person is forced to invent reasons for a
preference they can recognize but cannot neatly explain. In a 1991 experiment, [Timothy Wilson
and Jonathan Schooler](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2016668/) found that asking students to
analyze their reasons shifted choices away from expert ratings in their jam and course studies.
That is a warning about the wrong kind of analysis, not a license to skip analysis. When stakes,
data, or alternatives are large, a first response should open an investigation rather than close
one.

### The first impression carries history you cannot see

Snap judgments about people are especially dangerous because social cues can activate learned
associations before a person has considered them. The visible feeling may be a sense of ease,
threat, competence, or fit. Beneath it may sit cultural stereotypes, unequal prior exposure, or
the fact that the setting itself favors one kind of person. Treating such a response as neutral
turns an unexamined pattern into a decision rule.

## The Model

Thin-slicing names what occurs in the instant: a narrow sample produces a rapid impression. It
does not prescribe a cue audit, structured check, or feedback loop. Those deliberate practices
happen afterward, when they can test the impression against evidence and reveal whether confidence
is earned.

## Put it to work

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Name the domain before you trust the read">
When a judgment arrives quickly, write down the specific domain it belongs to. Ask how often you
have seen comparable cases and whether you learned what happened next. If there is no answer,
treat the reaction as a hypothesis, not evidence.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Find the cue, not the story">
Before acting, name the two or three observable details that drove the impression. A candidate
felt wrong is too vague. The candidate missed two concrete follow-up questions is a claim someone
else can inspect. If you cannot name a cue, postpone the verdict.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Install a pause for consequential choices">
Decide in advance which calls cannot be made on a first read: hiring, discipline, lending,
diagnosis, or any commitment that is hard to reverse. Add a second reviewer, a structured score,
or a cooling-off period before the decision reaches the point of no return.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Keep a calibration log">
For recurring decisions, record the initial judgment, the cues you used, and the eventual
outcome. Review a small set after the facts are known. This gives intuition the correction it
needs and exposes the situations where it only sounded sure.
</ExerciseCard>

## Where people get it wrong

<Callout kind="note">
The book's most useful claim is conditional, and it is easy to lose the conditions. The original
[thin-slice research](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12374446/) found variation in accuracy when
people judged brief nonverbal behavior, including lower accuracy under induced sadness in one
series of studies. Confidence therefore cannot certify a snap judgment: a later check can assess
whether the impression was useful, but it is not part of the thin slice. Nor should a framework
about quick cognition be used to excuse discrimination or to replace a fair procedure. In settings
with uneven power or high stakes, structure and review are safeguards, not signs that a decision
maker lacks instinct.
</Callout>

## If you remember one thing

Trust a fast judgment only after asking what trained it. Intuition is valuable when it is a
rapid impression that survives later checks, and risky when it is merely a quick feeling with no
way to be checked.

Thinking, Fast and Slow supplies a broader account of fast and slow judgment, Influence shows how
social cues can steer a response, Thinking in Bets separates decision quality from outcomes, and
Superforecasting turns later outcomes into a calibration practice.
