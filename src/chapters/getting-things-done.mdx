## The thesis

Put every open loop into a trusted system, clarify it into a next concrete action, and
give it a reliable home. Review that system often enough that attention can return to
choosing the work in front of you instead of rehearsing unfinished commitments.

## Why it matters

Work arrives as fragments: a request in a meeting, an email that implies three tasks,
a form that needs a missing number, an errand noticed on the way home. A single list
of mixed fragments does not answer the useful question, which is what can be done now.
The method separates intake from decision-making so a small incoming item does not
have to take over the moment in which it appears.

## Unmade decisions keep asking for attention

An unfinished commitment is not only a task. It is a question with no settled home:
What is this? Does it matter? What must happen next? Until those questions have an
answer and a place, the item keeps resurfacing whenever the mind has spare capacity.
The burden is often less the volume of work than the number of vague, uncontained
promises surrounding it. A trusted system makes those promises visible without asking
your attention to hold them all at once.

## A next action turns a wish into work

“Plan the workshop” is not an action. It names an outcome with several unknown moves
inside it. The useful question is smaller: what visible thing can be done to advance
it? Open the attendee spreadsheet, call the venue, or draft the agenda. That move may
be brief, but it changes the starting condition. If a commitment needs several moves,
keep the desired outcome on a projects list and put only the move that can begin now
on an action list.

## Organize for retrieval, not for admiration

The point of an organizing system is not to create a beautiful master list. It is to
make the right kind of information appear when it is useful. A date-specific promise
belongs on the calendar. A person who owes you something belongs on a waiting list.
Reference material should stop competing with actions, and an idea that is not a
commitment yet can sit in a someday list without pretending to be urgent. Each home
removes a different kind of search at the moment of action.

## Choose from the reality of the moment

Once the system holds clear actions, choosing does not require rethinking your whole
life every hour. First narrow the field by context: can this be done with the tool,
place, or person available? Then notice time and energy. A low-energy twenty-minute
gap is not a failure to use for demanding writing; it is a real constraint that can
make a short administrative action the sensible choice. Priorities still matter, but
they work better after the immediate limits have done their filtering.

## Review is what makes the system trustworthy

Externalizing commitments only helps if the system remains current enough to believe.
A regular review is the maintenance pass: empty capture points, scan projects for a
missing next move, look ahead at the calendar, and remove claims that no longer belong.
This is where vague intentions become choices again. Without the review, lists become
an archive of past optimism and the mind sensibly starts keeping its own backup copy.

## The Model

The five stages are not five apps or five separate moments in a day. They are distinct
kinds of attention. Capture collects what has your attention. Clarify decides its
meaning and any needed action. Organize gives the result a reliable home. Reflect puts
the whole picture back in view. Engage is the actual choice of what to do now. The
sequence loops because new commitments keep arriving while work is underway.

## Put it to work

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make one capture point unavoidable">
Choose one place for incoming commitments, such as a notebook, a phone inbox, or a
paper tray. For one week, put every task-shaped thought and every promise you make into
that place. Do not sort it while you are busy. The first win is proving that your head
does not have to be the collection tool.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Clarify one noisy item to its next move">
Pick the item that has been nagging at you most often. Write the outcome you want in a
short phrase, then name the next visible action. If the action takes only a moment and
you can do it now, finish it. Otherwise, put it where you will see it in the context
that makes it possible.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Build only the lists you will consult">
Start with a calendar, a short next-actions list, a projects list, and a waiting-for
list. Keep reference material elsewhere. Add categories only when they solve a real
retrieval problem. A smaller system that gets reviewed is more useful than an elaborate
one you avoid opening.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Set a recurring review appointment">
Reserve thirty minutes at the end of the week. Empty your capture point, update each
project with a next move, look two weeks ahead, and remove stale items. Treat this as
maintenance for the system, not as optional sorting when you happen to feel organized.
</ExerciseCard>

## Where people get it wrong

<Callout kind="note">
GTD is a method for managing commitments, not a machine for deciding which commitments
deserve your life. A perfectly maintained system can still hold too much work, the wrong
work, or work that belongs to somebody else. It can also become a form of productive
avoidance when list maintenance replaces action. Keep the structure light, let a calendar
hold only genuine time commitments, and use review to renegotiate obligations instead of
merely preserving them.
</Callout>

## If you remember one thing

Do not ask your mind to remember every unfinished promise. Give each one a trusted home,
make its next move clear, and review the system often enough that you can act from it
without rehearsing the whole backlog.
