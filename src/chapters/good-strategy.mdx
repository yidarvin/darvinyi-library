## The thesis

Strategy is not a wish list, a revenue target, or a polished statement of values. It is a
coherent response to a consequential difficulty. Richard Rumelt's test is demanding and
useful: make a diagnosis that brings the pivotal challenge into view, choose a guiding
policy for dealing with it, then take mutually reinforcing action. If any link is absent,
the organization may still be busy, but it has not yet made a strategy.

## Why it matters

Plans often fail before execution starts because they avoid the uncomfortable part: deciding
what the actual obstacle is and what will be left aside. Broad ambitions can gather support,
but they do not tell a team where to put scarce attention, money, or authority. A strategy
creates that focus. It gives people a shared reason to decline attractive work that does not
help with the problem at hand.

## Start with the difficulty, not the desired ending

An aspiration names a destination. A diagnosis explains the terrain between here and there.
That explanation does not need to capture every fact. Its job is to simplify a messy setting
enough to identify the obstacle that matters most. A hospital's challenge may be unreliable
handoffs, not merely a desire for better care. A software company's challenge may be slow
activation for a specific customer group, not a generic need to grow.

A diagnosis is provisional, so it should be exposed to evidence and revised when the facts
change. What it cannot be is decorative. If the description of the problem would fit every
organization in the industry, it is too vague to guide a choice.

## A guiding policy makes a choice about where to press

Once the difficulty is clear, a guiding policy supplies an approach. It is neither a detailed
task list nor a slogan. It says how the organization will gain traction: concentrate on one
segment, simplify the product, protect a scarce capability, change the sequence of work, or
otherwise exploit the shape of the situation. The test is whether the policy helps people
make a hard tradeoff without waiting for another meeting.

This is where strategy becomes uncomfortable. A policy that tries to preserve every option
usually spreads effort across too many fronts. A useful policy narrows the field, not because
narrowness is virtuous, but because attention and resources have limits.

## Coherence is a design problem

Actions become strategic when they work together. A new pricing model, an incentive plan,
and a service promise can each sound sensible on their own while pulling the organization in
different directions. Coherence asks a stricter question: does each action make the others
more likely to succeed? The answer should be visible in the operating design, not merely in
the presentation that describes it.

Coherence does not mean every decision is centrally controlled. It means local choices share
an organizing logic. Teams can adapt to what they learn while still recognizing which
dependencies they must protect and which actions would cancel the rest.

## Bring a distant ambition into working range

Long-term aims are important, but a team cannot act directly on "become the category leader"
or "transform the service." Rumelt's useful move is the proximate objective: a demanding
nearer target that sits within reach of current knowledge and resources. It turns an
overwhelming ambition into the next solvable problem without pretending that the larger aim
has disappeared.

A good proximate objective is concrete enough to assign, observe, and learn from. It is not
just an easier goal. It is the constraint whose removal opens the next move. For a company
with poor onboarding, that might be getting a new customer to a first successful outcome in
one session, before spending on broader acquisition.

## Look for leverage before adding effort

Leverage is a place where a limited intervention changes the situation more than the same
effort elsewhere. It may come from timing, a bottleneck, an underused capability, an
asymmetric cost, or the fact that one decision controls several downstream outcomes. Finding
it requires judgment about the system, not optimism about working harder.

The practical implication is to rank possible initiatives by their connection to the
diagnosis. A visible project can still be peripheral. A less glamorous fix can be pivotal if
it removes the constraint that holds several important activities back.

## The Model

The strategy kernel is an argument in motion. Diagnosis identifies the challenge that calls
for attention. Guiding policy selects the broad way of dealing with it. Coherent actions turn
that choice into coordinated work. The order matters because actions without a policy become
a list, and a policy without a diagnosis has no reason to be the right one.

## Put it to work

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Write the diagnosis in plain language">
Choose one decision that needs a strategy. Write three sentences: what outcome is at risk,
what constraint is causing the risk, and what evidence makes that constraint more plausible
than the alternatives. Ask someone close to the work where the diagnosis is vague or false.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Make one policy choice">
Complete this sentence: "For the next ninety days, we will address [challenge] by
[approach], rather than [two plausible alternatives]." The alternatives matter. They show
what the policy commits resources away from, and they expose whether the choice is real.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Test the action set for contradiction">
List the five most consequential actions already underway. For each, state which part of the
guiding policy it serves and what other action it depends on. Pause, change, or end anything
that cannot make that connection. A shorter list with clear reinforcement is stronger than a
longer list with private conflicts.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Choose the nearest hard objective">
Name the obstacle that must move before the larger ambition can become realistic. Set a result
to reach in the next six to twelve weeks, appoint an owner, and decide what evidence will
show that the obstacle really moved. When the result arrives, diagnose again before extending
the plan.
</ExerciseCard>

## Where people get it wrong

<Callout kind="note">
The kernel is a discipline for thinking, not a substitute for knowledge of a market,
technology, or institution. A neat diagnosis can still be wrong, and coherence can make a
team efficiently pursue the wrong policy. Treat the framework as a set of claims to test with
customers, operators, and evidence. It also does not settle every legitimate objective. A
strategy can describe how to pursue a choice; leaders still need judgment about whose
interests the choice serves and which constraints should not be traded away.
</Callout>

## If you remember one thing

Strategy earns the name when it confronts a real obstacle, makes a consequential choice about
how to meet it, and organizes action so the pieces reinforce one another. Everything else may
be useful planning, but it is not the same thing.
