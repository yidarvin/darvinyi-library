## The thesis

McKeown's argument is a discipline of selection, not a trick for packing more into a
day. Time and attention are finite, while plausible requests are nearly endless. The
work is to notice that conflict, decide what deserves a serious share of your effort,
and remove the rest before the rest makes the decision for you. Fewer commitments can
produce better work when they leave enough concentration, energy, and recovery for the
commitments that remain.

## Why it matters

Overload rarely arrives as one obviously bad choice. It accumulates through agreeable
meetings, projects with fuzzy owners, invitations accepted before thinking, and work
that feels urgent because somebody else has placed it in front of you. Each item can
seem harmless alone. Together they turn a calendar into a set of promises that compete
for the same limited person. Essentialism asks a blunt question before that accumulation
becomes normal: what contribution would be diminished if this time went elsewhere?

## A choice left unmade is still a choice

The starting point is agency. A request does not become a commitment merely because it
arrives in an inbox or because someone asks with confidence. People can pause, ask what
the work requires, and decide. Without that pause, other people's priorities become the
default organizer of a life. The cost is not only a crowded schedule. It is the gradual
loss of any visible connection between how time is spent and what a person meant to make
possible.

## Exploration is part of deciding

Selective work needs room to look before it commits. That can mean reading more widely,
asking a better question, trying a small version of an idea, or simply taking a walk
without a device. The point is not endless analysis. It is to give alternatives enough
attention that the best one has a chance to distinguish itself from the merely available
one. When every open space is already assigned, the next choice can only be a reaction.

## Every yes spends a scarce resource

The difficult fact behind prioritization is that appealing options can conflict. Choosing
one may mean declining another, postponing it, or admitting that it belongs to someone
else. Treating trade-offs as a failure encourages a fantasy in which everything stays
alive, then each promise receives a thin and unreliable slice of attention. Naming the
trade-off early is cleaner. It gives a chosen commitment enough capacity to become good,
rather than letting several commitments become perpetually almost finished.

## Raise the bar before you say yes

Most choices do not deserve equal consideration. A vague sense that an opportunity is
pretty good is a weak reason to commit weeks of work, a recurring meeting, or a portion
of a team's attention. A stronger filter asks what this opportunity advances, what it
will displace, and whether its value clears a threshold that justifies the cost. The
threshold need not be numerical, but it should be high enough that a polite request does
not pass simply because refusing feels awkward.

## Boundaries make the chosen work credible

Saying no is not an act of hostility. It is how a yes gains meaning. A clear boundary
protects a project from accidental expansion and gives other people an honest answer
before they build plans around your availability. The conversation can still be generous:
decline the request, offer a smaller form of help, point to someone better placed to do
it, or name a time when you can revisit it. What matters is that the answer fits the
priority rather than the momentary pressure.

## Make execution lighter than intention

Selection is only useful when the chosen work survives ordinary life. McKeown emphasizes
preparation: leave a buffer before deadlines, remove a recurring obstacle, and turn a
decision into a small routine so it does not need to be remade every morning. These are
not glamorous moves. They make plans less fragile. A calendar with no slack makes every
delay a crisis, while a protected buffer allows the important work to continue when the
week gets noisy.

## Put it to work

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Name the contribution that needs room">
Choose one result that would make the coming month meaningfully better: a decision,
piece of work, relationship, or recovery practice. Write what it needs from you in hours,
attention, and support. If the requirement is invisible, other commitments will spend
that capacity first.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Audit one recurring commitment">
Pick a meeting, project, or obligation that returns each week. Ask what it advances, what
it displaces, and what would happen if you stopped, shortened, delegated, or delayed it.
Make one concrete change, even if it is only a smaller trial for the next two weeks.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Use a pause before new commitments">
For the next seven days, do not accept non-urgent requests on the first conversation.
Say that you will check your current commitments and reply tomorrow. The pause creates
space for a real answer and makes it easier to see the trade-off.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Put a buffer beside the important work">
Find the deadline or appointment most likely to expand. Add a block before it for
preparation or after it for recovery and follow-up. Protect that block as part of the
commitment, not as optional empty time.
</ExerciseCard>

## Where people get it wrong

<Callout kind="note">
Doing less is not a license to abandon shared obligations or judge people whose time is
already constrained by care, money, health, or an employer's demands. Many commitments
cannot simply be declined, and a team cannot function if everyone guards only private
priorities. The useful application is often collective: clarify the few outcomes that
matter, reduce low-value work together, and make trade-offs visible to the people who
will carry their cost. Selectivity helps most when it is paired with responsibility and
fairness.
</Callout>

## If you remember one thing

Protect a small number of contributions that are truly worth your effort. The decisive
move is not working faster after every yes. It is choosing more carefully before the yes
is made.
