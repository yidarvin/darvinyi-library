## The thesis

Kahneman's useful provocation is that thought is not one uniform activity. Much of it is
rapid, automatic, and shaped by association. Another mode can inspect, calculate, compare,
and follow rules, but it costs attention and is often reluctant to take over. The result is
not random error. It is a recognizable family of mistakes: the mind answers an easier
question than the one it was asked, lets a first number pull later estimates, and trusts a
coherent story more readily than a representative sample.

## Why it matters

The most consequential choices rarely arrive with a warning label. Hiring, forecasting,
investing, diagnosis, and policy all ask for judgment under incomplete information. The
point is not to distrust every hunch. It is to notice which jobs can tolerate a quick
impression and which need a pause, a comparison class, or a method that makes individual
intuition less decisive.

## A good story is not a complete view

The mind is excellent at making what is in front of it feel sufficient. A vivid interview,
one striking anecdote, or a familiar name can produce a clean explanation before the missing
information has even been noticed. Confidence then tracks the neatness of the story, not the
size or quality of the evidence behind it. That is why a narrow data set can feel more
persuasive than a messy but relevant reference class.

## First numbers leave a trace

An arbitrary number can become a starting point for an estimate. Once it is present, thought
often adjusts away from it, but not far enough. A list price changes what a fair price feels
like. Last year's budget frames this year's negotiation. Even a number introduced as a rough
placeholder can bend a forecast. The repair is procedural: ask for independent estimates
before a shared number is discussed, then compare the range and the reasons behind it.

## Hard questions are quietly replaced

Some questions demand evidence that is slow to gather: Will this hire thrive here? How likely
is this plan to work? How much will this decision matter in five years? When the hard version
is uncomfortable or underspecified, the mind can answer a nearby, easier question instead.
Do I like this person? Does the proposal sound polished? Can I picture a success? The easier
answer arrives with a feeling of certainty, which disguises the substitution.

## Resemblance is not probability

People often judge likelihood by resemblance. A candidate who sounds like an archetypal
founder seems more likely to build a company; a small sample that looks orderly seems more
trustworthy than chance permits. The missing move is to ask how common the outcome is before
looking at the particular case. A distinctive description can be useful evidence, but it
cannot erase the background frequency of the thing being predicted.

## Experience and memory keep different records

The self that lives through an event and the self that later reports on it are not keeping the
same ledger. A remembered evaluation can put unusual weight on an intense moment and on how
an episode finished, while giving less weight to its duration. This matters whenever a team
uses retrospective ratings to design an experience. The survey may describe the ending more
than the whole journey. Pair it with measures collected along the way when duration and
burden matter.

## The Model

The two-system language is best treated as a map of jobs, not a diagram of two separate
machines in the skull. System 1 supplies impressions, associations, and practiced responses
with little felt effort. System 2 can hold rules in mind, inspect an argument, and override a
first response, but attention is limited. Good decision design does not demand that System 2
supervise every moment. It reserves deliberate checks for choices where a confident error is
expensive.

## Put it to work

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Separate the first estimate">
Before a planning meeting, have each person write a forecast privately, including the range
and the evidence used. Reveal the numbers together. This makes it harder for the first voice
in the room to become everyone else's anchor.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Name the reference class">
When a plan feels promising, find several genuinely comparable cases. Ask how often the
outcome occurred for that group before explaining why this instance is special. The base rate
is a starting point, not a veto.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Turn a verdict into a question">
If you catch yourself saying that a candidate, pitch, or plan just feels right, write the
decision question beside that feeling. Then list the evidence that would change the answer.
The gap often reveals the easier question that took over.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Build a pause into high-stakes choices">
Decide in advance which choices require a second look: large commitments, irreversible
changes, and forecasts that drive budgets. Use a short checklist, an outside reviewer, or a
cooling-off period. A pause is most useful when it is part of the process, not a heroic act
of willpower.
</ExerciseCard>

## Where people get it wrong

<Callout kind="note">
The two systems are a teaching model, not a claim that the brain contains two neatly bounded
agents. Fast judgment is not synonymous with bad judgment. In a stable domain with genuine
feedback, practiced intuition can be excellent; slow analysis can also be selective or
self-serving. Some famous laboratory findings associated with this period of psychology,
especially broad claims about social priming, have also proved less reliable in later
replication efforts. The durable lesson is more modest: errors have patterns, context
matters, and important decisions improve when their process invites calibration.
</Callout>

## If you remember one thing

Do not ask a first impression to do work it cannot do. When a decision matters, slow the
process enough to surface the missing evidence, the comparison class, and the question you
may have quietly replaced.
