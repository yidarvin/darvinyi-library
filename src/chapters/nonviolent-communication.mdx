## The thesis

Rosenberg's central move is to treat a difficult exchange as a problem of connection before
it becomes a problem of solutions. Instead of leading with a verdict about what another
person is, say what happened, name the feeling it brought up, identify the need involved,
and ask for a concrete action. The sequence does not promise agreement. It gives both people
enough usable information to look for an answer without making one person the enemy.

## Why it matters

Under stress, conversation contracts. A missed deadline becomes evidence that someone does
not care. A refusal becomes disrespect. Each person prepares a case while the other prepares
a defense, and the original issue gets buried under claims about character. Nonviolent
Communication, often shortened to NVC, offers a disciplined way to slow that escalation. It
asks speakers to separate what they saw from the meaning they assigned to it, and to make the
human stakes discussable.

## Start with what a camera could record

An observation is deliberately plain: something said, done, scheduled, or omitted that two
people could check. “The report arrived after the meeting began” is an observation. “You are
unreliable” is an interpretation with a verdict attached. The difference is not politeness
for its own sake. A verdict invites a fight over identity, while a concrete event gives the
conversation a shared object. This does not require you to pretend a pattern is harmless. It
requires giving the pattern a factual description before arguing about what it means.

## Feelings point beneath the argument

Feelings are information about how an event is landing, not evidence that someone else has
committed a crime against you. Saying “I am anxious” or “I am disappointed” reveals more
than “I feel ignored,” which smuggles in a conclusion about the other person's intent. The
feeling can then point toward what matters: reliability, consideration, rest, autonomy,
belonging, safety, or one of many other human needs. That turn matters because it changes
the question from who caused the pain to what the situation needs to hold.

## Needs are not the same as the plan

A need is the condition you are trying to protect. A strategy is one possible way of meeting
it. That distinction keeps a conversation from treating one proposed solution as the whole
of reality. “I need you to call me every evening” sounds like a need, but it joins two things:
the underlying wish for closeness or reassurance and one particular method for getting it.
When the need is named separately, more options appear. A short check-in, a shared calendar,
or a different rhythm might meet the same need without asking either person to surrender
their own.

## Empathy tries to understand before it solves

NVC asks for the same four kinds of attention when listening as when speaking. Hear the
event as the other person describes it, then test a guess about their feeling and need.
“Are you frustrated because you need more predictability?” is not a claim to know their
private world. It is an invitation to correct the guess. Advice, reassurance, explanation,
and comparison can all be useful later. In the first tense minutes they often interrupt the
thing the other person is still trying to make clear.

## A request leaves room for a real answer

The last step is an action someone can actually take, not a demand dressed in gentle words.
“Would you send the draft by Thursday afternoon?” is specific enough to accept, decline, or
revise. “Can you be more responsible?” leaves the other person guessing. The difference
shows up most clearly when the answer is no. A request treats no as information about a
constraint, then looks for another strategy. A demand treats no as disobedience and adds a
punishment, overtly or quietly. The aim is not to make refusal painless. It is to keep the
relationship and the problem visible after refusal arrives.

## The Model

The four parts work as a sequence, but not as a magic sentence. You may begin by listening
to the other person, return to the observation when the story gets fuzzy, or stop after the
need if no request is ready. The value of the model is its separation of kinds of information:
what happened, what it stirred, what matters, and what could be tried now. Keeping those
apart makes a hard conversation less likely to collapse into accusation or vague goodwill.

## Put it to work

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Write the camera version">
Before a conversation you are dreading, write one sentence that a camera or audio recorder
could confirm. Remove labels such as careless, selfish, or dismissive. If the sentence still
claims to know motive, keep editing until it describes an event, a time, or exact words.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Separate the need from your first solution">
Finish this sentence privately: “I am feeling ___ because I need ___.” Then write three
different ways that need might be met. Bring only one concrete request to the conversation,
but keep the alternatives in mind so a no does not leave you with nowhere to go.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Ask for something observable">
Turn a broad wish into an action, a time, and a choice. Rather than asking a colleague to
communicate better, ask whether they can post a two-line status by 3 p.m. on Tuesdays. Make
the request small enough that the answer tells you something useful.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Listen for the need in a no">
When someone declines, do not immediately restate your case. Ask what makes the request hard
to meet and listen for the need or constraint in the answer. Name what you heard, then see if
another strategy can protect both sides of the problem.
</ExerciseCard>

## Where people get it wrong

<Callout kind="note">
The framework can become a polished way to control another person. A speaker may use its
vocabulary while still insisting that their preferred strategy is the only acceptable one.
That is pressure in a softer voice. It also cannot make every conflict symmetrical. In a
relationship marked by coercion, intimidation, or violence, clearer self-expression does not
create safety or remove the need for boundaries and outside support. Use the model to seek
understanding where there is enough safety for a conversation, not as an obligation to keep
engaging with someone who is unsafe.
</Callout>

## If you remember one thing

When conflict begins, trade the judgment for four kinds of clarity: what happened, what you
feel, what matters, and what you are asking for now. A clear request may still receive a no,
but it gives both people a chance to solve the actual problem instead of defending their
character.
