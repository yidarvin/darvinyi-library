## The thesis

Forecasting is a skill: break broad questions into answerable parts, anchor an initial
probability in comparable cases, and state the odds openly. Revise those odds as evidence
arrives, then let a scored record expose and improve the judgment.

## Why it matters

Organizations often ask for forecasts when they are really asking for reassurance. A senior
person says a launch is likely to succeed, a committee calls a risk remote, or a report says
conditions are improving. Those phrases conceal both the chance being claimed and the point
at which it can be checked. A probability makes uncertainty discussable. A dated question
with a clear outcome makes a forecast accountable.

## Key ideas

### Start by making the question smaller

Big questions invite big stories. “Will this market recover?” can hold several different
claims about demand, policy, timing, and what recovery even means. A forecaster first pins
down the outcome, deadline, and relevant comparison class. Then the question can be broken
into drivers that are less mysterious than the original whole. The purpose is not to make a
complex event simple. It is to give each uncertainty a place where evidence can change it.

### Begin outside the story

The inside view starts with the particulars that feel vivid now: a charismatic founder, a
fresh policy, a plausible plan. The outside view asks what happened in similar cases before
those particulars were known. That base rate is not a verdict. It is an anchor against the
mind's habit of treating the present case as unprecedented. Start with the comparison class,
then move away from it only for evidence that genuinely distinguishes this case.

### Use a number, not a foggy phrase

“Possible,” “likely,” and “a fair chance” sound informative until two people attach very
different odds to the same words. A percentage does not remove judgment, but it exposes the
judgment for discussion. Is 70 percent really more defensible than 55? What evidence would
move it? The number also preserves useful distinctions. Treating 51 percent and 99 percent
as the same prediction, because both are called yes, throws away most of what the forecaster
knows.

### Update in small, visible steps

A first estimate is a starting position, not a promise to defend. New reporting, an observed
decision, a failed prerequisite, or a missing deadline can all change the odds. The useful
move is to say what changed, adjust by an amount that matches its weight, and leave a record
of the revision. This avoids two opposite errors: freezing at the opening estimate, and
swerving with every headline. Good forecasters retain a view while holding it loosely.

### Keep score across many questions

Forecasting skill cannot be read from one dramatic call. An event can resolve in your favor
for the wrong reasons, and a sensible probability can lose to an unlikely outcome. The test
comes from a long record of forecasts that later resolve. Scoring rules reward being close
to what happened and penalize both overconfidence and empty hedging. Calibration is the
plain-language check: outcomes assigned about 70 percent should occur around seven times in
ten over a large enough set.

### Make disagreement do useful work

Groups can turn into an echo chamber, especially when a confident person supplies the first
answer. The better version of collaboration keeps individual estimates visible, asks people
to name their evidence, and treats disagreement as a search for information that someone
else missed. Combining independent views can reduce individual blind spots, while challenge
keeps the combined view from becoming a polite average. A team helps when it creates more
honest comparison, not when it manufactures consensus.

## Put it to work

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Turn one prediction into a question">
Pick a decision you expect to revisit within six months. Write an outcome that can be checked,
the date by which it will resolve, and what counts as yes. If the question cannot be scored,
make it narrower until it can.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Set an outside-view starting point">
Before reading the latest news, name three to ten comparable cases and write a rough base
rate. The comparison will be imperfect. That is fine. Its job is to prevent a fresh narrative
from becoming your only evidence.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Make an update ledger">
Record your current percentage, the two strongest reasons for it, and the next fact that
would shift it. When that fact arrives, move the number and write one sentence explaining
why. Do not erase the old estimate.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Review the resolved calls">
At the end of the period, collect the questions that resolved. Look for a pattern rather than
a flattering anecdote: were high probabilities too high, low probabilities too low, or were
you repeatedly missing one kind of driver? Change the process before making the next set of
calls.
</ExerciseCard>

## Where people get it wrong

<Callout kind="note">
Superforecasting is not a claim that the future can be mastered, or that a percentage tells
you what to do. A forecast describes uncertainty; a decision also needs stakes, values,
constraints, and a plan for losses. The evidence behind the approach comes largely from
many dated geopolitical questions in a tournament, where outcomes and scores were available.
That supports a disciplined way to make and improve forecasts. It does not guarantee that a
small team can foresee a singular crisis, estimate an event with no useful comparison class,
or transfer a good track record unchanged to every domain. Explicit probabilities can still
be badly wrong. Their advantage is that wrongness becomes visible enough to study.
</Callout>

## If you remember one thing

Treat every forecast as a revisable probability, not a badge of confidence. Break the
question down, begin with the base rate, write the odds, and let a scored record teach you
where your judgment needs to move.
