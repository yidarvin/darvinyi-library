## The thesis

Cialdini's central claim is that people do not examine every request from first principles.
They rely on quick social rules that are usually useful: return a favor, act in line with a
commitment, follow credible peers, heed expertise, listen to people you trust, and protect
an opportunity that may disappear. A request can attach itself to one of those rules and
make agreement feel natural before its merits have been considered. The practical lesson is
two-sided. Make requests in ways that respect the other person's judgment, and pause when a
request seems to be borrowing its force from something beside the decision itself.

## Why it matters

Most consequential persuasion is ordinary. It happens in a sales conversation, a hiring
meeting, a family favor, a fundraising email, or a product screen. The same shortcuts that
save attention can also make a deadline, a title, a favor, or a crowd do more work than the
actual offer deserves. Seeing the pattern creates a small but valuable gap between the cue
and the answer.

## A request can activate a shortcut

No one can inspect every choice in full. When attention is thin or the answer is unclear,
the mind uses familiar signals as evidence about what to do. That is sensible much of the
time. A trusted expert may know more than you do, and a colleague's experience may be
relevant. Trouble begins when the signal is real but irrelevant, or when it is staged to
stand in for reasons that are missing.

## A favor can create a social debt

Reciprocity turns giving into a relationship rule. A genuinely helpful act can make a later
request easier to hear because cooperation is easier to sustain when help is returned. The
same rule can be recruited by an unwanted sample, an extravagant opening ask, or a gift that
arrives before you have agreed to anything. The useful distinction is simple: gratitude is
appropriate, but it does not make the eventual request a good one. Treat the favor and the
decision as separate questions.

## Small commitments can pull later choices into line

People tend to prefer a story of themselves that hangs together. Once you have taken a
position, especially one that is public or effortful, the next compatible action can feel
like proof that the first one was sincere. This is useful when a small commitment supports a
value you actually hold. It becomes a trap when the first step is trivial and the later one
is not. Before extending a commitment, name the new cost, the new scope, and whether you
would choose it if no earlier promise existed.

## Other people can become evidence

Social proof is strongest when the situation is ambiguous and the people you see resemble
you in a way that matters. A long queue, a set of peer testimonials, or a colleague's choice
can provide information that you do not have. It can also create a loop in which everyone is
copying everyone else. Ask what the group knows, whether the examples are genuinely similar,
and whether the behavior is being presented honestly. A crowd is information, not a verdict.

## Source cues are not the same as reasons

Liking and authority make a messenger easier to follow for different reasons. Liking rests
on affinity, familiarity, warmth, and shared identity. Authority rests on relevant knowledge,
responsibility, or demonstrated skill. Both can be useful filters. Neither is a substitute
for checking the claim. A friendly person can be wrong, and a title can exceed the person's
expertise. Separate the source's relationship to you from the evidence supporting the ask.

## Loss can make an offer feel larger

Scarcity changes attention by making the cost of missing out vivid. A genuine limit can be
important: there may be one available seat, a real deadline, or a resource that cannot be
replaced. Yet a timer, an exclusive label, or a shrinking stock count can also turn urgency
into theater. The defense is a pause long enough to ask what is limited, why it is limited,
and whether you would want the offer if it remained available tomorrow.

## The Model

The original framework groups six common routes by which a request gains momentum:
reciprocity, commitment and consistency, social proof, liking, authority, and scarcity. They
are not six steps in a script, and a strong request need not use any of them. They are
different kinds of cues that can tilt an evaluation toward agreement. The model is most
useful as a diagnostic map. When you feel an automatic yes, identify which cue is carrying
the weight, then return to the merits of the request.

## Put it to work

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Name the cue before the answer">
When a request feels unusually easy to accept, pause and write the cue you notice: a favor,
a prior commitment, a crowd, a relationship, an expert, or a deadline. Then write one reason
the request stands on its own. If you cannot find one, delay the decision.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Separate a favor from a purchase">
If someone has done something for you, thank them plainly. Then evaluate any request that
follows by your own criteria: need, price, alternatives, and timing. A favor can deserve
appreciation without purchasing power over the next decision.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Make social proof specific and true">
When persuading ethically, use examples that are real, relevant, and close to the audience's
actual situation. Do not pad a count, manufacture testimonials, or describe a harmful norm
as if it were common. Evidence that cannot survive a follow-up question is not evidence.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="04" title="Install a scarcity check">
For any time-limited offer, ask three questions before acting: What exactly runs out? Who set
the limit? What would I choose with a day to compare alternatives? A real constraint will
still make sense after those questions.
</ExerciseCard>

## Where people get it wrong

<Callout kind="note">
These principles describe tendencies, not buttons that guarantee compliance. Their force
depends on context, the relationship, the stakes, and the evidence available to the person
being asked. The original book's model has six principles; Cialdini's later work adds unity
to a broader seven-principle formulation. More importantly, the framework can be used to
recognize manipulation as easily as to improve a request. A persuasive act earns its place
when the recipient would still judge it fair after seeing exactly how it was designed.
</Callout>

## If you remember one thing

When a request feels compelling, find the social cue that made it feel that way. Then decide
as if the favor, crowd, title, deadline, and friendly messenger were not doing the thinking
for you.
